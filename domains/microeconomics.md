# Microeconomics

## Scarcity and opportunity cost

Resources are finite; choices have opportunity costs — the value of the next-best alternative forgone. Every "yes" is a stack of implicit "nos".

## Supply and demand

Price emerges from the interaction of buyers' willingness-to-pay (demand) and sellers' willingness-to-sell (supply). At equilibrium, quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. Shifts in either curve move the equilibrium.

## Marginal thinking

Decisions should be evaluated at the margin, not in totals. The relevant question is "what does one more unit cost vs. produce?", not "what does the whole project cost?". Sunk costs are irrelevant going forward.

## Elasticity

Elasticity measures how responsive quantity is to price. A 1% price increase that causes a 5% drop in quantity is elastic demand; a 1% drop is inelastic. Necessities, monopolies, and addictive goods tend to be inelastic.

## Comparative advantage

Two parties gain from trade when each specializes in what they produce at the lowest opportunity cost — even if one party is absolutely better at everything. Comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, drives trade.

## Externalities

Costs or benefits that fall on third parties not party to a transaction. Pollution is a negative externality; herd immunity from vaccination is a positive one. Markets systematically over-produce negative externalities and under-produce positive ones.

## Public goods

Goods that are non-rivalrous (one person's use doesn't reduce another's) and non-excludable (you can't stop free-riders). National defense, lighthouses. Markets under-supply public goods because no one can capture the full benefit.
