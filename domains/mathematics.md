# Mathematics

## Proof

A mathematical proof is a chain of logical inferences from agreed-upon axioms to a conclusion. The standard isn't "convinces most readers" — it's "every step is valid." Proof is what distinguishes mathematics from every other empirical discipline.

## Sets and functions

A set is an unordered collection of distinct objects. A function maps each element of one set (the domain) to exactly one element of another (the codomain). Almost every mathematical structure is built on top of these two primitives.

## Number systems

Naturals (counting) → integers (subtraction closure) → rationals (division closure) → reals (limits) → complex (square roots of negatives). Each level adds an operation the previous level couldn't close under. The progression itself is a worked example of mathematical motivation.

## Limits

The formal idea behind "approaches but never reaches." Underlies calculus, real analysis, and continuity. Epsilon-delta: for every error tolerance ε you give me, I can find an input range δ such that the output stays within ε of the limit.

## Derivatives

The derivative of a function at a point is the slope of its tangent line — the instantaneous rate of change. Built on limits. Powers calculus, optimization, physics.

## Linear algebra

The mathematics of vectors and linear transformations. Vectors are arrows; matrices are functions that send arrows to arrows. Everything from least-squares regression to neural networks to quantum mechanics runs on this substrate.

## Probability

Probability assigns numbers between 0 and 1 to events, measuring uncertainty. Two axiomatic frameworks dominate: Kolmogorov (measure-theoretic, frequentist) and Cox (logical extensions of Boolean truth values, Bayesian). Both yield the same calculus; they differ on interpretation.

## Bayes' rule

P(H | E) = P(E | H) × P(H) / P(E). Updates a prior probability P(H) into a posterior P(H | E) given new evidence E. The single most important equation in applied probability — and the cure for base-rate neglect.
