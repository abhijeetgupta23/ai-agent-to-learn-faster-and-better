# Persuasion

## Reciprocity

People feel obligated to return favors. Free samples, unsolicited gifts, and small unrequested concessions trigger a reciprocity reflex — even when the recipient didn't want the original gesture. Cialdini's foundational principle.

## Commitment and consistency

Once a person commits — especially publicly — they feel pressure to act consistently with that commitment. The foot-in-the-door technique exploits this: a small initial yes makes a later larger yes far more likely.

## Social proof in persuasion

We follow the crowd as a shortcut for "what's correct" — especially under uncertainty or in unfamiliar situations. Testimonials, queue length, "10,000 customers can't be wrong" all leverage social proof.

## Liking

People are persuaded more by those they like. Liking comes from similarity, compliments, cooperation, and familiarity. Tupperware parties weaponized this — sales by friends of friends outperformed direct sales by orders of magnitude.

## Authority

People defer to perceived authority. Titles, uniforms, and credentials transfer some of the audience's critical thinking to the authority figure. Milgram's obedience experiments dramatize the dark side.

## Scarcity in persuasion

People assign higher value to things they perceive as rare or disappearing. "Limited time offer," "only 3 left in stock," and the velvet rope all exploit this. Built on loss aversion from psychology.

## Framing

The same information persuades differently depending on how it's framed. "90% survive" vs. "10% die" elicits different choices though they're equivalent. Framing is the persuasion lever closest to outright manipulation.

## Ethical line

Each of these levers can be used to align someone's actions with their own interests (a doctor framing surgical risk) or to extract value against their interests (a scammer using urgency). The technique is amoral; the application is not.
