# Game Theory

## Players, strategies, and payoffs

A game has players (decision-makers), strategies (the actions each can take), and payoffs (the outcome each player gets given every combination of strategies). Game theory studies rational choice when outcomes depend on other actors' choices.

## Dominant strategy

A strategy that yields a higher payoff than any alternative, regardless of what others do. Rational players pick dominant strategies when they exist. Most interesting games don't have one.

## Nash equilibrium

A combination of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing strategy — given what everyone else is doing. Equilibrium does not mean optimal; the prisoner's dilemma equilibrium is mutually destructive.

## Prisoner's dilemma

Two players each get a better individual payoff from defecting, regardless of what the other does — but mutual defection is worse for both than mutual cooperation. Cornerstone illustration that individual rationality can produce collective irrationality.

## Repeated games

The prisoner's dilemma changes when played repeatedly with the same partner. Now reputations, trust, and punishment matter. Tit-for-tat — cooperate first, then mirror your opponent's last move — is robust across tournaments.

## Zero-sum vs. positive-sum

In zero-sum games, one player's gain exactly equals another's loss (poker). In positive-sum games, the total can grow (trade, knowledge sharing). Conflating the two is a common error in negotiation.

## Schelling points

In coordination games without communication, players gravitate to focal points that are prominent for cultural or psychological reasons. "Meet in NYC at noon" → most people pick Grand Central. Schelling points are the substrate of social conventions.
