# Ethics

## Consequentialism

Right action is whatever produces the best outcomes. Utilitarianism — maximize aggregate well-being — is the most influential variant. Powerful framework, but vulnerable to repugnant conclusions (utility monsters, justified atrocities for great net gains).

## Deontology

Right action follows from duties and rules, regardless of outcomes. Kant's categorical imperative: act only on maxims you could will to be universal law. Don't lie even to save a life. The strength is moral inviolability; the weakness is moral rigidity.

## Virtue ethics

The right action is what a person of good character would do. Goodness lies in stable dispositions — courage, honesty, practical wisdom — rather than in rules or outcomes. Aristotle is the classical source; revived by Anscombe and MacIntyre.

## The trolley problem

A runaway trolley will kill five people; you can divert it onto a track where it kills one. Should you? Most say yes (consequentialist intuition). Variant: push a large stranger off a bridge to stop the trolley, killing him to save five. Most say no (deontological intuition). The discrepancy is the puzzle.

## Moral luck

Two drivers, identical recklessness; one happens to hit a pedestrian, the other doesn't. We judge them differently — but their characters and choices were identical. Moral luck challenges the idea that moral responsibility tracks only what's in our control.

## Is–ought gap

Hume's observation that you cannot derive a normative conclusion ("ought") purely from descriptive premises ("is"). Empirical facts about psychology or economics don't tell you what you should do. Recognizing this gap is the entry fee for serious ethical reasoning.

## Applied ethics

Translating frameworks into specific domains: medical ethics (consent, end-of-life), business ethics (whistleblowing, externalities), AI ethics (alignment, fairness). Applied ethics is where the rubber meets the road and frameworks reveal their tradeoffs.
